Research

Research Overview

Disruptive mutations in the gene CHD8 (Chromodomain Helicase DNA-binding protein 8) are highly associated with risk of autism spectrum disorder (ASD)—more than a dozen damaging mutations have been identified in CHD8 from unrelated individuals diagnosed with ASD (Fig. 1).

CHD8 affects DNA accessibility of gene promoters by binding to H3K4 methyl groups and remodeling nucleosomes (Fig. 2). Additionally, CHD8 regulates expression of ASD risk-associated networks during neurodevelopment, and disruption of CHD8 function can alter expression of the genes in these networks. While CHD8 function has been largely studied in the cortex, its role in other brain structures has been largely unexplored. Our research aims to characterize the molecular and circuit-level changes associated with loss of CHD8 function. Specifically, we are interested to understand how loss of CHD8 affects genes, cell types, and regulatory networks critical for neurodevelopment.

Determine genomic regions and cell types altered by loss of CHD8

We aim to identify the effects of Chd8 reduction on cell types and lineages in the developing mouse brain. Using snRNAseq and snATACseq analysis in embryonic and postnatal developmental timepoints, we are determining the genome-wide expression and epigenetic profiles that define the distinct brain cell populations in WT and Chd8+/- mice.

Generate chromatin maps of distinct cell types to assess genetic pathways regulated by CHD8

We are using an in vivo inducible biotinylation system to map the CHD8 regulatory networks at cellular resolution. We have generated a Chd8-tag mouse line in which a FLAG-Avi epitope is incorporated at the 3' end of the endogenous Chd8 gene to encode a Chd8-tag protein. Activation of biotin ligase BirA using cell type specific Cre drivers of the developing brain will biotinylate Chd8-tag only in those cell types. Biotin-streptavidin pulldowns followed by bioChIPseq allows us to map Chd8 binding targets at cellular resolution. We are also performing conventional RNAseq and ChIPseq by isolating cells expressing GFP activated by the same Cre drivers to further define the transcriptional and epigenetic state of the cells used in bioChIPseq.
